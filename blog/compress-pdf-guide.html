How to Compress a PDF: 3 Proven Methods

June 14, 2026 · 5 min read · ← Back to Blog

PDF files can quickly grow in size — especially when they contain high-resolution images, embedded fonts, or scanned pages. A 20-page scanned document can easily exceed 50MB, making it hard to email, upload, or store.

In this guide, we'll walk through three methods to compress PDFs effectively and explain how our free compression tool works under the hood.

Method 1: Image Re-Compression (Best for Scanned PDFs)

When a PDF is created from scanned images, most of the file size comes from the embedded images. Re-compressing these images at a lower quality level is the most effective way to reduce file size.

The compression tool in PDFTools Pro uses this approach. It renders each page as a JPEG image at a controlled quality level and embeds the compressed version back into a new PDF. Here's how the three levels compare:

💡 Compression Levels:
Low (85% quality): ~10-20% smaller — visually lossless, best for final documents
Medium (60% quality): ~30-50% smaller — good balance for everyday use
High (35% quality): ~60-80% smaller — maximum reduction, ideal for archiving

Method 2: Remove Unnecessary Elements

Before compressing, consider stripping out elements that add bulk without value:

  • Unused fonts — Embedded fonts can add megabytes. Our tool uses standard fonts.
  • Duplicate images — Multiple copies of the same logo or icon waste space.
  • Annotations and metadata — Comments, stamps, and edit history add up.
  • Blank pages — Remove them before compressing for extra savings.

Method 3: Reduce Resolution

Many PDFs contain images at 300 DPI or higher — far more than needed for screen viewing. Reducing to 150 DPI (screen quality) can cut file size significantly while still looking sharp on displays.

Our PDF → Images tool lets you extract pages at different DPI settings (72, 150, or 300), then you can rebuild the PDF at a lower resolution.

File Size Guidelines

As a general rule of thumb:

  • Under 1MB: Ideal for email attachments
  • 1-5MB: Good for most web uploads
  • 5-10MB: Acceptable for internal sharing
  • Over 10MB: Consider compressing further
🔒 Privacy Note: All compression happens locally in your browser. Your files never leave your device. This is especially important when compressing sensitive documents like contracts or personal records.

When NOT to Compress

Avoid compression when:

  • The PDF will be professionally printed (keep 300 DPI)
  • The document contains fine text at small sizes
  • The file is already under 500KB
  • You need to preserve every pixel of embedded images
